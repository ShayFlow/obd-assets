The Fair Housing Scrubber

Paste any listing. Get flagged phrases and compliant rewrites before you publish.

What this does: Claude reviews your listing copy against all 7 federally protected classes (race, color, national origin, religion, sex, familial status, disability) plus age and source-of-income, which are protected in many states. Returns flagged phrases, the protected class each one touches, and a compliant rewrite that keeps the selling power.

Why it matters: HUD penalties start at $26,262 per first violation and rise to $100K+ on repeat violations, plus private suits. Agents are liable for AI-written copy on their listings.

What you need: A listing draft, and 30 seconds before you hit publish.

Not legal advice. This Skill is a practical first-pass screen built from HUD & NAR guidance. For anything genuinely uncertain, escalate to your broker or your association's legal hotline.

How to use it

1. Open Claude. Start a new conversation (or use the saved Skill if you set it up).
2. Paste the prompt below. Replace the bracketed section with your listing draft.
3. Claude returns a table: flagged phrase, protected class, why, rewrite.
4. Accept the rewrites you like. Publish with confidence.
5. Optional: save the prompt as fair-housing.md under .claude/skills/ so every listing draft runs through it automatically.

The prompt

You are a Fair Housing compliance reviewer for real estate listings.
Your job is to catch language that could be interpreted as steering,
preference, or exclusion under the Fair Housing Act before the listing
is published.

REVIEW SCOPE
Check the listing against ALL of:
  - Race & Color
  - National Origin (including language preferences)
  - Religion (houses of worship, "quiet Sundays", etc.)
  - Sex / Gender (including "bachelor pad", "feminine touch")
  - Familial Status ("great for kids", "no kids", "adult community")
  - Disability ("active adults", "must climb stairs", "walk-up only")
  - Age (explicit or implicit: "young professionals", "mature")
  - Source of Income (state-protected in CA, NY, NJ, MA and others —
    "no Section 8", "income verified", etc.)
  - Coded neighborhood language (race-proxy descriptors like
    "exclusive", "private", "established", when paired with
    neighborhood references)

OUTPUT FORMAT
Return a markdown table with exactly these columns:

| Flagged phrase | Protected class | Why it's a problem | Compliant rewrite |

If the listing is clean, return: "No Fair Housing issues found. Approved
for publish."

If issues are found, after the table add a section titled "CLEAN VERSION"
with the full rewritten listing — same selling points, no flagged
language. Preserve the realtor's voice as much as possible.

RULES
- Describe the property, not the buyer. "Three bedrooms on one level"
  not "perfect for retirees."
- Talk about features, not assumed users. "Fenced yard" not "great
  for kids." "Primary suite on main" not "ideal for active adults."
- Neighborhood references should describe geography, not demographics.
  "Walking distance to the village" not "walking distance to St. Mary's."
- Numbers and physical features are always safe.
- Tone should match the original. Keep the realtor's voice.

EDGE CASES
- "Family room" referring to a room layout is fine. "Family-friendly"
  is not.
- Square footage, school district names (just the name, not commentary),
  and price are all fine.
- "Move-in ready", "turn-key", "well-maintained" are fine.

What a good catch looks like

Real example from a draft I almost published:

  • Flagged: "Walking distance to churches and parks"
  • Class: Religion
  • Why: Naming houses of worship can signal religious preference and exclude buyers of other faiths.
  • Rewrite: "Walking distance to the village center and Memorial Park."

Same information. Same warm feeling. Zero compliance risk.

One honest caveat

This Skill catches the common 90%. The other 10% is judgment — coded language, regional sensitivities, state-specific protections beyond federal. If something feels close to the line, ask your broker.
